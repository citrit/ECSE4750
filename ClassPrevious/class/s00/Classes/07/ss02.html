Interview With Nvidia's David Kirk
Gamecenter had the opportunity to talk with David Kirk, chief scientist at Nvidia, about the GeForce 256, Nvidia's latest graphics chip.

Gamecenter: For starters, what's your educational background, and how did you end up at Nvidia?

David Kirk: I went to school at M.I.T. and studied mechanical engineering and then computer graphics. From there I worked on building CAD workstations. Then I went back to school at Caltech and got a Ph.D. in computer science. After that I worked at Crystal Dynamics, the video game developer, then went straight on to Nvidia, where I've been for a little more than three years.

GC: What are the key differences between the GeForce 256 and Nvidia's earlier chip, the RIVA TNT2?

D. K.: There are a few major differences. One is in the raw texturing and fill-rate engine. The TNT2 does two pixels per clock; the GeForce does up to four pixels per clock. The GeForce also has a higher bandwidth memory interface and integrates hardware support for geometric transform and lighting for both OpenGL and Direct3D APIs.

GC: Could you explain what the geometry engine is and how it works?

D. K.: If you're drawing triangles to a screen, they are provided in a coordinate space that is relative to a model that is built, and they have to be transformed to the point of view that you see on the screen. That process is very mathematically intensive and, in order to be precise, needs to be performed in floating point. So there's a very high horsepower floating point engine that does the transformation.

The way to think about this is to consider the case of a model of a car that is in some position, and on the screen it's spinning. You do the transformation to translate and rotate the points from the position that the car is modeled in into the orientation that it is in for each frame as it's spinning.

"The GeForce is approximately three to five times faster than a 500-MHz Pentium III that is doing the same tasks."
After the transform process, each vertex of each triangle that is drawn needs to have lighting performed on it. In order for you to see the surface of the object and what the object looks like, you position lights in the scene, and you calculate for each point what the amount of reflection is for each light.

GC: If a game took perfect advantage of the GeForce geometry engine, what kind of speed boost would we see?

D. K.: It turns out that in a pre-GeForce system, you are bottlenecked by a number of things in the system for a typical game or application. You are bottlenecked by the amount of the transformation and lighting processing that the CPU can do, as well as...by the frontside bus and the AGP bus. The GeForce offloads computation processing from the CPU and allows fewer round-trips for the data to be copied across those two other buses.

If the application has simply transformation and no hardware lighting, then the GeForce is approximately 3 to 5 times faster than a 500-MHz Pentium III that is doing the same tasks. If you're doing up to 8 lights, the GeForce can be up to 20 to 30 times faster than a Pentium III.
