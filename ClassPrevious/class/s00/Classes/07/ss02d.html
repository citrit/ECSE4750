Interview With Nvidia's David Kirk
GC: What resolution do you see as the sweet spot for gaming this year and next year?

D. K.: I think I've seen a continuing trend upward. I'm hoping to see a lot more 1,280 by 1,024 moving up to 1,600 by 1,200 by the beginning of next year.

GC: Let's postulate that around the holiday season, users can buy a GeForce with geometry acceleration, or a product that has considerably higher fill rate, but no geometry processing. Which should they buy, and why?

D. K.: It depends on what they like to do. If they want to play the leading-edge games, they're going to need geometry processing, because a lot of the games coming out during the next 6 months are going to be pushing significantly higher levels of geometry. We've been working with about 20 developers for about a year to develop either custom versions or configuration options in games to provide up to 10 times the geometry of last year's games.

Without a GeForce-class product, you will not be able to play those games with those geometry options turned on. It's very clear that for characters and for model detail, there is a night-and-day difference with more geometry than with less geometry. Pushing resolution one notch higher is definitely better, but it's not a night-and-day kind of difference.

"Before the PlayStation2 ships in volume, PC products will far exceed its performance."
GC: Looking at next year and the year after, what are the big trends in 3D graphics?

D. K.: The big trend I can see--as we (Nvidia) continue to push integration and are able to do more and more on a single chip--is that we will continue to crawl our way up the graphics pipeline. We will do more and more on the graphics chip. We will also add a lot more flexibility and programmability into the graphics pipeline so that we can accommodate some alternative rendering paradigms doing drastically different kinds of pictures than what we were able to do before.

We will also continue to push the trend of geometry. The GeForce is capable of doing 15 million polygons per second, which is a great number, but...easily within a year we will be able to do four to eight times that number. We can begin to think about other kinds of geometry representations by then. We will work with the API developers to expose that to developers in enough time so they can support it in their games.

One of the things implicit in what you're asking involves the PlayStation2, which will have peak specs significantly above what PC specs have been recently. I think that before the PlayStation2 ships in volume, PC products will far exceed the performance of the PlayStation2.

GC: Thanks very much for taking the time to talk with us. We look forward to testing the GeForce for ourselves.
